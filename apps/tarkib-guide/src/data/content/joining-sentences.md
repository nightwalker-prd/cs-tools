# Guide to Tarkib: Sentence-Level Constructions (التَّرْكِيبُ الجُمَلِي)

## Overview

This guide covers **sentence-level Tarkib** — the grammatical structures used for vocative expressions, oaths, conditional sentences, and related constructions. These are distinct from phrase-level Tarkib (such as descriptive or possessive phrases) and deal with how complete sentences are structured and connected.

---

## 1. Vocative Sentences (النِّدَاءُ)

The vocative construction is used to call out to or address someone.

### Key Terms

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| نِدَاءٌ | nidā' | Vocative Sentence |
| حَرْفُ النِّدَاءِ | ḥarf al-nidā' | Particle of Vocation |
| جَوَابُ النِّدَاءِ | jawāb al-nidā' | Sentence After Vocative |

### Structure

A vocative sentence consists of three parts:

```
حَرْفُ النِّدَاءِ + المُنَادَى + جَوَابُ النِّدَاءِ
Vocative Particle + The One Called + Response Sentence
```

### Common Vocative Particles

The most common particle is **يَا** (yā) meaning "O!" or "Hey!"

### Examples

| Arabic | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| يَا مُحَمَّدُ، اجْلِسْ | O Muhammad, sit down! |
| يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ | O people! |

---

## 2. Oath Sentences (القَسَمُ)

Oaths are used to emphasize the truthfulness of a statement.

### Key Terms

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| قَسَمٌ | qasam | Oath |
| جَوَابُ القَسَمِ | jawāb al-qasam | Sentence After Oath |

### Structure

An oath sentence consists of:

```
أَدَاةُ القَسَمِ + المُقْسَمُ بِهِ + جَوَابُ القَسَمِ
Oath Particle + What is Sworn By + The Sworn Statement
```

### Examples

| Arabic | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| وَاللهِ لَأَفْعَلَنَّ ذٰلِكَ | By Allah, I will certainly do that |
| تَاللهِ لَقَدْ آثَرَكَ اللهُ عَلَيْنَا | By Allah, Allah has certainly preferred you over us |

## 3. Conditional Sentences (الشَّرْطُ)

Conditional sentences express "if-then" relationships and are one of the most important constructions in Arabic.

### Key Terms

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| أَدَاةُ الشَّرْطِ | adāt al-sharṭ | Conditional Conjunction |
| شَرْطٌ | sharṭ | Condition Clause (the "if" part) |
| جَوَابُ الشَّرْطِ | jawāb al-sharṭ | Result Clause (the "then" part) |
| فَاءٌ رَابِطَةٌ | fā' rābiṭa | فَ added to connect the result clause |
| لَامُ الْجَوَابِ | lām al-jawāb | لَ added to the result clause |

### Structure

```
أَدَاةُ الشَّرْطِ + شَرْطٌ + جَوَابُ الشَّرْطِ
Conditional Tool + Condition + Result
```

### Conditional Particles (حُرُوفُ الشَّرْطِ)

These are particles (not nouns) used to introduce conditions:

| Arabic | English | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| إِنْ | if | Most common; for real/possible conditions |
| لَوْ | if | For hypothetical/unreal conditions |
| لَوْلَا | if not / were it not for | Expresses contrary-to-fact conditions |

### Conditional Nouns (أَسْمَاءُ الشَّرْطِ)

These are nouns that introduce conditions. Unlike particles, they carry meaning and have grammatical positions:

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| مَنْ | man | whoever (for people) |
| مَا | mā | whatever (for things) |
| أَيْنَ | ayna | wherever (for place) |
| مَتَى | matā | whenever (for time) |
| أَيَّانَ | ayyāna | whenever (for time) |
| كَيْفَ | kayfa | however (for manner) |
| أَنَّى | annā | from wherever / however |
| كَمْ | kam | however many (for quantity) |
| أَيٌّ | ayy | whichever (general) |

### The Connecting فَ (فَاءٌ رَابِطَةٌ)

When the result clause (جَوَابُ الشَّرْطِ) begins with certain types of sentences, the particle **فَ** must be added to connect it to the condition. This is called **فَاءٌ رَابِطَةٌ** (the connecting fā').

### Examples

| Arabic | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| إِنْ تَدْرُسْ تَنْجَحْ | If you study, you will succeed |
| مَنْ يَعْمَلْ خَيْرًا يَجِدْهُ | Whoever does good will find it |
| أَيْنَ تَذْهَبْ أَذْهَبْ مَعَكَ | Wherever you go, I will go with you |

## 4. Command and Prohibition Responses

### Sentence After a Command (جَوَابُ الأَمْرِ)

When a command (أَمْرٌ) is followed by a consequence or result.

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| أَمْرٌ | amr | Command |
| جَوَابُ الأَمْرِ | jawāb al-amr | Sentence After Command |

### Sentence After a Prohibition (جَوَابُ النَّهْيِ)

When a prohibition (نَهْيٌ) is followed by a consequence or result.

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| نَهْيٌ | nahy | Prohibition |
| جَوَابُ النَّهْيِ | jawāb al-nahy | Sentence After Prohibition |

### Examples

| Arabic | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| اِجْتَهِدْ تَنْجَحْ | Strive and you will succeed |
| لَا تَكْذِبْ يُحِبَّكَ النَّاسُ | Don't lie (and) people will love you |

## 5. Explanatory Sentences

### Reason Sentence (جُمْلَةٌ تَعْلِيلِيَّةٌ)

A sentence that explains the **reason** or cause for something.

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| جُمْلَةٌ تَعْلِيلِيَّةٌ | jumla ta'līliyya | Sentence that shows reason |

### Corrective Sentence (جُمْلَةٌ اسْتِدْرَاكِيَّةٌ)

A sentence that **corrects** a presumption or removes a misunderstanding.

| Arabic | Transliteration | English |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| جُمْلَةٌ اسْتِدْرَاكِيَّةٌ | jumla istidrākiyya | Sentence that removes a presumption |

---

## 6. Verb-Like Particles (حُرُوفٌ مُشَبَّهَةٌ بِالْفِعْلِ)

These particles resemble verbs in their function and affect the sentence structure:

| Arabic | Transliteration | English | Function |
|--------|-----------------|---------|----------|
| لَعَلَّ | la'alla | perhaps / so that | Expresses hope or expectation |
| لٰكِنَّ | lākinna | however / but | Expresses contrast or correction |

These particles are called "verb-like" because they take a noun (in the accusative) and a predicate, similar to how verbs work.

## Summary

Understanding sentence-level Tarkib is essential for:

1. **Reading comprehension** — Recognizing the structure helps parse complex sentences
2. **Writing** — Constructing grammatically correct conditional, vocative, and oath sentences
3. **Translation** — Understanding how Arabic sentence structures map to English

The key constructions are:

| Type | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **نِدَاءٌ** (Vocative) | Addressing someone |
| **قَسَمٌ** (Oath) | Emphasizing truth |
| **شَرْطٌ** (Conditional) | Expressing if-then relationships |
| **أَمْرٌ / نَهْيٌ** (Command/Prohibition) | Giving orders with consequences |
| **تَعْلِيلِيَّةٌ** (Explanatory) | Showing reasons |
| **اسْتِدْرَاكِيَّةٌ** (Corrective) | Correcting presumptions |

Each construction has its own particles, structure, and rules that must be followed for proper Arabic sentence composition.
