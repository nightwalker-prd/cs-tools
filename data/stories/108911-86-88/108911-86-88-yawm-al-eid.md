# (٣١) يَوْمُ الْعِيْدِ — The Day of Eid

**Source:** Pages 86–88

---

## Arabic Text & English Translation

### Paragraph 1

> كَانَ أَمْسِ يَوْمُ الْعِيدِ ، اجْتَمَعَ النَّاسُ وَالأَطْفَالُ عِنْدَ الْغُرُوبِ يَتَرَاءَوْنَ الْهِلَالَ ، وَصَعِدُوا عَلَى سُقُوفِ الْبُيُوتِ وَالسُّطُوحِ وَعَلَى الْمَنَارَاتِ.

Yesterday was the day of Eid. The people and children gathered at sunset to sight the crescent moon. They climbed onto the roofs of the houses, the terraces, and the minarets.

### Paragraph 2

> ظَهَرَ الْهِلَالُ فَهَتَفَ الأَوْلَادُ: «الْهِلَالُ ، الْهِلَالُ» وَجَرَوْا إِلَى بُيُوتِهِمْ وَسَلَّمُوا عَلَى آبَائِهِمْ وَأُمَّهَاتِهِمْ وَعَلَى الأَقَارِبِ ، فَدَعَوْا لَهُمْ بِالْبَرَكَةِ وَطُولِ الْعُمُرِ.

The crescent appeared, and the children shouted: "The crescent! The crescent!" They ran to their homes and greeted their fathers, mothers, and relatives, who prayed for them with blessings and long life.

### Paragraph 3

> وَنَامَ الأَطْفَالُ لَيْلَةَ الْعِيدِ قَلِيلاً ، وَاسْتَيْقَظُوا مُبَكِّرِينَ وَقَدْ نَظَرُوا إِلَى مَلَابِسِهِمْ وَأَحْذِيَتِهِمْ وَقَلَانِسِهِمْ مِرَاراً عَدِيدَةً.

The children slept little on the night of Eid. They woke up early, having looked at their clothes, shoes, and caps many times over.

### Paragraph 4

> وَلَمَّا كَانَ صَبَاحُ الْعِيدِ قَامُوا مِنْ فُرُشِهِمْ وَصَلَّوُا الصُّبْحَ وَاغْتَسَلُوا وَغَيَّرُوا مَلَابِسَهُمْ ، وَلَبِسَ كَثِيرٌ مِنْهُمْ مَلَابِسَ جَدِيدَةً وَأَحْذِيَةً جَدِيدَةً وَقَلَانِسَ جَمِيلَةً ، وَرَأَتْهُمْ أُمَّهَاتُهُمْ فَفَرِحْنَ بِهِمْ ، وَقَدَّمَ لَهُمْ آبَاؤُهُمْ وَأَقَارِبُهُمْ جَائِزَةَ الْعِيدِ.

When the morning of Eid came, they got up from their beds, prayed the dawn prayer, bathed, and changed their clothes. Many of them wore new clothes, new shoes, and beautiful caps. Their mothers saw them and were delighted with them, and their fathers and relatives gave them the Eid gift.

### Paragraph 5

> وَكَانَ وَلَدٌ يَتِيمٌ تَخْدِمُ أُمُّهُ فِي بَيْتِ سَعِيدٍ وَلَيْسَ عِنْدَهُ لِبَاسٌ جَدِيدٌ وَلَا حِذَاءٌ جَدِيدٌ وَلَا قَلَنْسُوَةٌ نَظِيفَةٌ ، فَاغْتَسَلَ وَلَبِسَ لِبَاسَهُ الْقَدِيمَ وَقَدْ تَشَقَّقَ ، وَلَبِسَ قَلَنْسُوَتَهُ الْقَدِيمَةَ وَقَدْ تَوَسَّخَتْ ، وَكَانَ يَنْظُرُ إِلَى أَتْرَابِهِ وَإِلَى أَوْلَادِ الأَغْنِيَاءِ بِغِبْطَةٍ وَخَجَلٍ ، وَكَانَتْ أُمُّهُ مَحْزُونَةً تَذْكُرُ أَبَاهُ.

There was an orphan boy whose mother worked as a servant in Sa'id's house. He had no new clothes, no new shoes, and no clean cap. So he bathed and put on his old clothes, which were torn, and wore his old cap, which had become dirty. He would look at his peers and at the children of the wealthy with envy and shame. His mother was sad, remembering his father.

### Paragraph 6

> حَزِنَ سَعِيدٌ بِهَذَا الْمَنْظَرِ وَاسْتَحْيَى فِي نَفْسِهِ فَأَسْرَعَ إِلَى صُنْدُوقِهِ وَأَهْدَى إِلَيْهِ مَلْبُوسًا نَظِيفًا وَقَلَنْسُوَةً نَظِيفَةً ، فَذَهَبَ الْيَتِيمُ وَغَيَّرَ اللِّبَاسَ وَفَرِحَ كَثِيرًا وَفَرِحَتْ أُمُّهُ ، وَدَعَتْ لِسَعِيدٍ بِالْبَرَكَةِ وَطُولِ الْعُمُرِ.

Sa'id was saddened by this sight and felt ashamed within himself, so he hurried to his trunk and gave him clean clothes and a clean cap. The orphan went and changed his clothes and was very happy, and his mother was happy too. She prayed for Sa'id with blessings and long life.

### Paragraph 7

> وَلَمَّا ارْتَفَعَتِ الشَّمْسُ خَرَجَ النَّاسُ إِلَى الْمُصَلَّى وَكَانَ مَنْظَرًا جَمِيلًا يَقُولُونَ: «اللهُ أَكْبَرُ اللهُ أَكْبَرُ ، لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ ، وَاللهُ أَكْبَرُ اللهُ أَكْبَرُ ، وَلِلَّهِ الْحَمْدُ».

When the sun rose, the people went out to the prayer ground, and it was a beautiful sight. They were saying: "God is Greatest, God is Greatest; there is no god but God; and God is Greatest, God is Greatest, and to God belongs all praise."

### Paragraph 8

> وَصَلَّى الإِمَامُ بِالنَّاسِ ، ثُمَّ خَطَبَ ، وَرَجَعَ النَّاسُ مِنَ الْمُصَلَّى بِطَرِيقٍ آخَرَ ، وَزَارَ النَّاسُ بَعْضُهُمْ بَعْضًا وَضَيَّفَ بَعْضُهُمْ أَصْدِقَاءَهُمْ ، وَهَنَّأَ كُلُّ مُسْلِمٍ صَدِيقَهُ وَقَالَ: «عِيدٌ سَعِيدٌ» كُلَّ عَامٍ وَأَنْتُمْ بِخَيْرٍ.

The imam led the people in prayer, then gave a sermon. The people returned from the prayer ground by a different route. People visited one another, and some hosted their friends. Every Muslim congratulated his friend and said: "Happy Eid! May you be well every year."

### Paragraph 9

> وَكَانَ صَبَاحُ الْعِيدِ جَمِيلًا ، وَفِي الْعَصْرِ ذَكَرَ النَّاسُ رَمَضَانَ وَفُطُورَهُ ، وَفِي اللَّيْلِ ذَكَرُوا التَّرَاوِيحَ ، وَشَعَرُوا كَأَنَّهُمْ فَقَدُوا شَيْئًا أَوْ ضَاعَ مِنْهُمْ شَيْءٌ ، وَقَالَ بَعْضُ النَّاسِ: «الْعِيدُ سَاعَاتٌ وَرَمَضَانُ كُلُّهُ عِيدٌ».

The morning of Eid was beautiful. In the afternoon, the people remembered Ramadan and its iftars (breaking of the fast), and at night they remembered the Tarawih prayers. They felt as if they had lost something, or as if something had gone missing from them. Some people said: "Eid lasts only hours, but all of Ramadan is an Eid."

## Grammatical Analysis (تحليل نحوي)

### 1. كَانَ and its Sisters (كان وأخواتها)

The text makes heavy use of **كان** (was) as a *nāsikh* (abrogator) that enters upon a nominal sentence, giving the subject (اسم كان) رفع and the predicate (خبر كان) نصب:

- **كَانَ أَمْسِ يَوْمُ الْعِيدِ** — *يومُ* is اسم كان (marfūʿ). *أمسِ* functions adverbially.
- **كَانَ صَبَاحُ الْعِيدِ جَمِيلاً** — *صباحُ* is اسم كان (marfūʿ), *جميلاً* is خبر كان (manṣūb).
- **كَانَ وَلَدٌ يَتِيمٌ** — Here كان is existential ("there was"), with *وَلَدٌ* as its subject (indefinite, acceptable because كان is existential here).
- **كَانَ مَنْظَراً جَمِيلاً** — *منظراً* is خبر كان (manṣūb). The اسم is implied (the scene/situation).
- **كَانَتْ أُمُّهُ مَحْزُونَةً** — *أمُّهُ* is اسم كان, *محزونةً* is خبر كان.
- **لَيْسَ عِنْدَهُ لِبَاسٌ جَدِيدٌ** — ليس is a sister of كان. *لباسٌ* is اسم ليس (marfūʿ), *عندهُ* is خبر ليس (a prepositional phrase).

### 2. لَمَّا (Temporal Conjunction — "when")

Used twice with the same structure — **لمّا + كان + جملة فعلية**:

- **وَلَمَّا كَانَ صَبَاحُ الْعِيدِ قَامُوا** — "When the morning of Eid came, they got up..."
- **وَلَمَّا ارْتَفَعَتِ الشَّمْسُ خَرَجَ النَّاسُ** — "When the sun rose, the people went out..."

لمّا is a *ẓarf zamān* (adverb of time) that takes a past-tense verb and requires a *jawāb* (response clause).

The text is rich with derived verb forms, showing how Arabic builds meaning from roots:

### 5. The فَ Connector (فاء السببية / فاء العاطفة)

The text uses فَ extensively to show **cause-and-effect** or **immediate sequence**:

- **ظَهَرَ الْهِلَالُ فَهَتَفَ الأَوْلَادُ** — The crescent appeared, *so* the children shouted (cause → effect)
- **فَدَعَوْا لَهُمْ** — *so* they prayed for them (result of the greeting)
- **فَاغْتَسَلَ وَلَبِسَ** — *so* he bathed and wore (result of having no new clothes)
- **فَأَسْرَعَ إِلَى صُنْدُوقِهِ** — *so* he hurried to his trunk (result of feeling ashamed)
- **فَفَرِحْنَ بِهِمْ** — *so* they (the mothers) were delighted (result of seeing the children)

This contrasts with **وَ** (and), which simply conjoins without implying causation.

### 6. Hal Clause (الحال)

- **وَقَدْ نَظَرُوا إِلَى مَلَابِسِهِمْ** — "having (already) looked at their clothes..." This is a *ḥāl* (circumstantial) clause introduced by **وَقَدْ** + past tense, describing the state of the children when they woke up.
- **وَقَدْ تَشَقَّقَ** — "and it had become torn" (ḥāl clause describing the state of the old clothes)
- **وَقَدْ تَوَسَّخَتْ** — "and it had become dirty" (ḥāl clause describing the cap)

### 7. كَانَ + مضارع (Past Continuous / Habitual)

- **كَانَ يَنْظُرُ إِلَى أَتْرَابِهِ** — "He used to look at / was looking at his peers"
- **يَقُولُونَ: اللهُ أَكْبَرُ** — In context with كان: "They were saying: God is Greatest"

This construction (كان + imperfect) expresses continuous or habitual past action.

### 8. Iḍāfa Chains (الإضافة)

The text has many possessive/genitive constructions:

- **يَوْمُ الْعِيدِ** — "the day of Eid" (2-part iḍāfa)
- **سُقُوفِ الْبُيُوتِ** — "the roofs of the houses"
- **جَائِزَةَ الْعِيدِ** — "the gift of Eid"
- **أَوْلَادِ الأَغْنِيَاءِ** — "the children of the rich"
- **طُولِ الْعُمُرِ** — "length of life" (i.e., long life)
- **صَبَاحُ الْعِيدِ** — "the morning of Eid"
- **بَعْضُ النَّاسِ** — "some of the people"

Note: The first word (muḍāf) loses its tanwin and definite article; the second (muḍāf ilayhi) is always in the genitive (majrūr).

### 9. Negation with لَا and لَيْسَ

- **لَيْسَ عِنْدَهُ لِبَاسٌ جَدِيدٌ** — ليس (verbal negation with كان-like grammar)
- **وَلَا حِذَاءٌ جَدِيدٌ وَلَا قَلَنْسُوَةٌ نَظِيفَةٌ** — Repeated لا for emphasis: "nor new shoes, nor a clean cap"
- **لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ** — لا here is لا النافية للجنس (لا that negates the entire genus), putting إله in the accusative.

### 10. Reciprocal Construction (بعضهم بعضاً)

- **زَارَ النَّاسُ بَعْضُهُمْ بَعْضًا** — "The people visited one another"
- **ضَيَّفَ بَعْضُهُمْ أَصْدِقَاءَهُمْ** — "Some of them hosted their friends"

The بعض...بعضاً construction is the standard Arabic way to express reciprocal action ("each other").

### 11. كَأَنَّ (as if)

- **شَعَرُوا كَأَنَّهُمْ فَقَدُوا شَيْئًا** — "They felt *as if* they had lost something"

كأنّ is one of إنّ وأخواتها (inna and its sisters); it puts its subject (الاسم) in the accusative and its predicate (الخبر) in the nominative. Here هُمْ is the attached pronoun as its subject, and the verbal sentence فقدوا شيئاً is the predicate.

## Thematic Notes

This story illustrates several cultural and religious concepts:

1. **Moon sighting (رؤية الهلال):** The communal tradition of looking for the crescent moon to confirm the start of Eid.
2. **Eid greetings and prayers:** Children greeting elders, who respond with prayers for blessings and long life.
3. **Charity and generosity (إحسان):** Sa'id's compassion for the orphan — giving from his own belongings.
4. **The Eid prayer (صلاة العيد):** Going to the muṣallā, the takbīr, the sermon, and returning by a different route (a Prophetic tradition).
5. **Nostalgia for Ramadan:** The bittersweet feeling of missing Ramadan's spiritual atmosphere, captured in the closing proverb: *"Eid lasts only hours, but all of Ramadan is an Eid."*
