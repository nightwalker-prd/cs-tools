# (٢٥) شَرٌّ وَخَيْرٌ — Evil and Good

**Poet:** أبو العَتاهِيَة (Abu al-Atahiya, d. 211 AH / 826 CE)

## Grammatical Analysis (تحليل نحوي)

### 1. Sentence Structure

The poem is built primarily on **nominal sentences** (جمل اسمية), which convey universal, timeless truths — fitting for a wisdom poem.

#### Lines 1–4: Paired Nominal Sentences

Each line presents two nominal sentences contrasting شرّ (evil) with خير (good):

**شَرُّ الْمَقَالِ الْكَذِبُ**
- **شَرُّ** — mubtada' (مبتدأ), marfūʿ, first term of iḍāfa
- **الْمَقَالِ** — muḍāf ilayh (مضاف إليه), majrūr
- **الْكَذِبُ** — khabar (خبر), marfūʿ
- Pattern: `Subject (iḍāfa) + Predicate`

**الْبُخْلُ عَيْبٌ فَاضِحٌ**
- **الْبُخْلُ** — mubtada', marfūʿ (definite with ال)
- **عَيْبٌ** — khabar, marfūʿ (indefinite — a flaw)
- **فَاضِحٌ** — ṣifa (adjective/نعت) of عَيْبٌ, marfūʿ, indefinite matching its described noun

### 2. The Iḍāfa Construction (الإضافة)

The poem makes heavy use of iḍāfa (possessive/genitive construction):

| Iḍāfa | Meaning | Type |
|---|---|---|
| شَرُّ الْمَقَالِ | the worst of speech | Superlative iḍāfa |
| خَيْرُ الْخِصَالِ | the best of traits | Superlative iḍāfa |
| طَهَارَةُ الْأَخْلَاقِ | purity of morals | Masdar iḍāfa |
| كَرَمِ الْأَعْرَاقِ | nobility of lineage | Masdar iḍāfa |
| شَرُّ شِيمَةٍ | the worst disposition | Superlative iḍāfa |
| حَذَرِ الْفَوَاتِ | fear of missing out | Masdar iḍāfa |

**Key rule:** The muḍāf (first noun) loses its tanwīn and definite article. Definiteness is inherited from the muḍāf ilayh.

### 3. إنَّ and Its Governance (Line 5)

**إِنَّ الْبَخِيلَ لَا يُحَبّ**
- **إنَّ** — particle of emphasis (حرف توكيد ونصب)
- **الْبَخِيلَ** — ism of إنّ (اسم إنّ), manṣūb (accusative)
- **لَا يُحَبّ** — khabar of إنّ (خبر إنّ), a verbal sentence
  - **لَا** — negation particle
  - **يُحَبّ** — muḍāriʿ verb, passive voice (مبني للمجهول), marfūʿ

### 4. Defective Noun: قَاضٍ (Line 3)

**الْعَقْلُ قَاضٍ عَادِلٌ**
- **قَاضٍ** is a defective noun (اسم منقوص) — its final yā' (ي) is dropped when indefinite in the nominative and genitive cases
- Full form would be: قَاضِيٌ → but tanwīn replaces the yā', giving قَاضٍ
- Here it is khabar (predicate), marfūʿ by estimated ḍamma on the deleted yā'

### 5. Imperative Verbs (Lines 8–10)

| Verb | Root | Form | Analysis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **تَأَنَّ** | أ ن ي | V (تَفَعَّلَ) | Imperative of تَأَنَّى — "be deliberate." The final alif maqṣūra is dropped in the imperative. |
| **اعْجَلْ** | ع ج ل | IV (أَفْعَلَ) | Imperative of أَعْجَلَ — "hasten!" Built on the jussive stem with sukūn. |
| **لَا تَكُ** | ك و ن | I | Apocopated form of لَا تَكُنْ (jussive of كَانَ with لَا الناهية). The نون is dropped for poetic lightness. |

### 6. لَا سِيَّمَا Construction (Line 8)

**لَا سِيَّمَا السُّرُورِ**
- **لا سيّما** — "especially, particularly" — a fixed expression
- **السُّرُورِ** — in the genitive (مجرور), treated as muḍāf ilayh after ما (when ما is treated as a relative pronoun or redundant)
- This is a classical construction meaning: "be deliberate in all matters, *especially* joyful ones"

### 7. Poetic License (ضرورة شعرية)

**مَالَكَ غَيْرُ نَفْسِكَا / لَا تَكُ عَنْهَا مُمْسِكَا**
- The final alif on **نَفْسِكَا** and **مُمْسِكَا** is added for rhyme (إشباع) — the kasra of the كَ suffix is elongated into an alif. This is a well-known poetic convention.
- **لَا تَكُ** — shortened from لَا تَكُنْ (the nūn of كان is dropped). This is common in both poetry and the Quran (e.g., فَلَا تَكُ فِي مِرْيَةٍ — Hūd:17).

### 8. Passive Voice (المبني للمجهول)

**لَا يُحَبّ** (Line 5)
- Root: ح ب ب
- Active: يُحِبُّ (he loves) — Form IV
- Passive: يُحَبُّ (he is loved)
- The ḍamma on the yā' and fatḥa on the second radical signal the passive pattern of the muḍāriʿ

### 9. Rhyme Scheme (القافية)

The poem shifts between three rhyme patterns:

| Lines | Rhyme | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| 1–4 | ـبُ / ـحٌ / ـلٌ | Alternating with -bu, -ḥun, -lun endings |
| 5 | ـبّ | Doubled bā' |
| 6 | ـاقِ | -āqi (genitive) |
| 7 | ـمَة | -ma(tun) |
| 8 | ـورِ | -ūri (genitive) |
| 9 | ـاتِ | -āti (genitive) |
| 10 | ـكَا | -kā (poetic elongation) |

## Thematic Summary

Abu al-Atahiya, the famous ascetic poet of the Abbasid era, delivers a series of moral aphorisms in compact, balanced couplets. The poem contrasts:

- **Lying** vs. **good manners** (line 1)
- **Miserliness** vs. **generosity** (line 2)
- **Intellect** vs. **vanity** (line 3)
- **Life's transience** and **wealth's impermanence** (line 4)

He then pivots to direct advice: pursue righteousness, maintain pure morals, avoid gossip and treachery, act with deliberation, and rush toward good deeds before the opportunity passes. The final couplet is a striking reminder: *you own nothing but yourself — so do not withhold even that from doing good*.
