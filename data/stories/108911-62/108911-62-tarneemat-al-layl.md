## Full Verse Translation (Poetic Rendering)

> Indeed the bed is soft and fair,
> In it you sleep without a care.
>
> Sleep, O my darling, safe and sound,
> Sleep safely — peace is all around.
>
> The day has gone and slipped from sight,
> With it went toil and weariness of light.
>
> And night draws near with gentle peace,
> Sleep safely — let your worries cease.
>
> The singing birds have found their rest,
> In God's protection — the Eternal, the Blessed.
>
> He who never neglects a soul,
> Sleep in His shelter, safe and whole.
>
> Sleep safely till the break of dawn,
> From every harm and grief withdrawn.
>
> Sleep in the refuge of mankind's Creator,
> Sleep in His protection — none is greater.

## Grammatical Analysis (تحليل نحوي)

### 1. إنَّ الفِراشَ النّاعِمَا — The use of إنَّ

- **إنَّ** is one of the حروف المشبهة بالفعل (particles resembling verbs). It puts its subject (اسم إنّ) in the **accusative** (منصوب).
- **الفراشَ** = اسم إنّ منصوب (accusative noun of إنّ)
- **الناعِمَا** = نعت/صفة منصوب (adjective in accusative agreeing with الفراش). The final alif (ا) is a **poetic license** (إشباع) — the standard form would be الناعِمَ. The vowel fatḥa is lengthened to an alif to maintain the meter and rhyme scheme (دائمَا / آمِنَا).

### 2. نَمْ — Imperative of a Hollow Verb (فعل أجوف)

- **نَمْ** is the أمر (imperative) of **نامَ - يَنامُ** (to sleep), a **hollow verb** (أجوف واوي — the middle radical is و).
- The wāw drops in the imperative form: نامَ → نَمْ
- Pattern: فعل أمر مبني على السكون

### 3. آمِنًا — The Ḥāl (حال)

- **آمِنًا** functions as a **حال منصوب** (accusative adverb of state/manner).
- It describes the **state** of the addressed person while sleeping: "sleep **[while being] safe**."
- Derived from the root أ م ن — it is an اسم فاعل (active participle) from أَمِنَ (to be safe/secure).
- The tanwīn fatḥa (ـًا) marks the accusative case of the ḥāl.

### 4. راحَ النَّهارُ واحتَجَبْ — Past Tense Narrative

- Two **past tense verbs** (فعل ماضٍ) connected by **و العطف** (conjunction of coordination):
  - **راحَ** (went away) — Form I of ر و ح. Here used in its meaning of "departed/went away" (not as a كان sister).
  - **احتَجَبَ** (hid, disappeared) — **Form VIII** (افتعل) of ح ج ب (to veil). The ta' infixes after the first radical: حَجَبَ → احْتَجَبَ.
- **النهارُ** = فاعل مرفوع (nominative subject) of both verbs.
- The final consonant is written without vowel (سكون) for the **rhyme scheme** (وقف/pause) — standard poetic convention.

### 5. واللَّيلُ بالأمنِ اقتَرَبْ — Form VIII Verb

- **اقتَرَبَ** = Form VIII (افتعل) of ق ر ب (to be near). Meaning: "drew near, approached."
- **بالأمنِ** = جار ومجرور (prepositional phrase: "with safety/security") — describes the manner in which night approached.
- **الليلُ** = مبتدأ or فاعل مقدّم (the night — nominative subject).

### 6. باتَتْ عصافيرُ الغَرَدِ — The Verb بات

- **باتَ** is one of **كان وأخواتها** (sisters of kāna) — specifically, it means "to spend the night in a state" or "to become at nighttime."
- **عصافيرُ** = اسم بات مرفوع (nominative noun of بات).
- **الغَرَدِ** = مضاف إليه مجرور — "birds **of singing**" = "singing birds."
- The implied خبر بات (predicate) is the prepositional phrase **في حفظِ مولانا** ("in the care of our Lord").

### 7. في حِفْظِ مَوْلانا الصَّمَدِ — Iḍāfa Chain (إضافة)

- **Double إضافة** (possessive chain):
  - حِفْظِ ← مُضاف (protection of...)
  - مَوْلانا ← مضاف إليه (our Lord) — itself contains: مَوْلى + نا (our)
- **الصَّمَدِ** = بدل or نعت (appositive or adjective) for مولانا — a Divine Name meaning "the Eternal, Self-Sufficient, upon whom all depend."

### 8. مَنْ لَيْسَ يَغفُلُ عن أحَدٍ — Relative Clause with Negation

- **مَنْ** = اسم موصول (relative pronoun: "the One who / He who")
- **لَيْسَ** = فعل ماضٍ ناقص / حرف نفي (negation — from the كان sisters). Here it negates the following imperfect verb.
- **يَغْفُلُ** = فعل مضارع مرفوع (present tense verb: "neglects / is heedless")
- **عن أحدٍ** = جار ومجرور (prepositional phrase: "of anyone")
- Complete meaning: "He who is never heedless of anyone" — a description of Allah.

### 9. في حِمى بارِي البَشَرِ — Triple Iḍāfa

- **Triple إضافة** (three-level possessive chain):
  - حِمى ← sanctuary/protection (of)
  - بارِي ← the Creator (of) — اسم فاعل from بَرَأَ (to create)
  - البَشَرِ ← mankind
- Full meaning: "in the sanctuary of the Creator of mankind."
- **بارِي** is one of Allah's names (البارئ — the Originator/Creator).

### 10. Poetic & Rhetorical Features

| Feature | Example | Explanation |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **إشباع** (vowel lengthening) | الناعِمَا، دائِمَا، آمِنَا | Final fatḥa extended to alif for meter/rhyme |
| **التصريع** (matching hemistichs) | الناعِمَا / دائِمَا | Both hemistichs of the opening line share the same rhyme |
| **الترديد** (refrain/repetition) | نَمْ آمِنَاً نَمْ آمِنَا | Repeated as a soothing lullaby refrain throughout |
| **السجع** (prose rhyme) | احتَجَبْ / التَّعَبْ / اقتَرَبْ | Internal rhyme within stanza |
| **الجناس** (paronomasia) | الأمن / آمِنَا | Words from the same root أ م ن used in different forms |
| **التكرار** (repetition) | نَمْ (appears 12+ times) | Creates the rhythmic, hypnotic quality of a lullaby |
| **الطباق** (antithesis) | النهار / الليل | Day vs. night — contrasting opposites |

### 11. Meter (البحر)

The poem follows a simplified, child-friendly meter close to **الرَّجَز** (rajaz) — the most common meter for didactic and children's poetry in Arabic. Each hemistich has approximately 3 feet (تفعيلات), with the pattern مُسْتَفْعِلُنْ or a variation thereof. The short, rhythmic lines make it ideal as a lullaby.

## Theme & Context

This is a **lullaby** (ترنيمة / تهليلة) addressed to a child at bedtime. The poem moves through three stages:

1. **The physical setting** (vv. 1-2): The soft bed is ready; sleep safely.
2. **The transition from day to night** (vv. 3-4): Day's hardships have departed; night brings peace.
3. **Divine protection** (vv. 5-8): Even the birds rest under God's care. The child is urged to sleep under the protection of الصمد (the Eternal) and بارِي البشر (the Creator of mankind) — the One who never neglects anyone.

The poem reflects a common motif in Islamic children's literature: trust in God's watchfulness (حفظ الله) as the source of nighttime security. The repeated refrain نَمْ آمِنًا functions both as a soothing rhythmic device and as a theological statement — true safety comes from God's protection.
